Frequently Asked Questions: GCC Setup in India

What is a Global Capability Center (GCC) and why are companies setting them up in India?

A Global Capability Center (GCC) — also called a captive center or in-house center — is a wholly owned subsidiary of a multinational corporation, set up in a lower-cost country to deliver business functions, technology, analytics, or operations for the parent company. India has become the world's leading GCC destination due to its deep talent pool, English-language proficiency, cost competitiveness, and mature technology ecosystem. As of 2024, India hosts over 1,700 GCCs, with the number growing rapidly as companies move beyond IT to set up GCCs for finance, supply chain, HR, marketing, and advanced analytics.

How long does it take to set up a GCC in India?

A realistic GCC setup timeline — from decision to first hire — is typically 9 to 18 months, depending on the scope and complexity. Legal entity registration in India takes 2 to 4 months. Leadership hiring adds another 3 to 6 months. Standing up the first cohort of functional staff takes a further 3 to 6 months. Companies that rush this timeline tend to make expensive mistakes in location choice, entity structure, and leadership hiring that can take years to correct. A good GCC advisory partner will help you move decisively without cutting corners.

Which city in India is best for setting up a GCC?

Bangalore, Hyderabad, and Pune are India's proven GCC hubs — with deep talent pools, mature real estate markets, and well-established MNC ecosystems. They are the natural starting point for many searches, particularly for technology-heavy functions. Emerging Tier 2 cities like Ahmedabad, Coimbatore, Nagpur, and Kochi offer compelling advantages in talent stability and cost for the right function mix. The right answer depends on your specific function mix, talent requirements, and retention priorities — a structured location analysis typically takes 4–6 weeks and produces a scored, business-case-linked recommendation.

What is the difference between a GCC and outsourcing?

Outsourcing means contracting a third-party provider to deliver a service — you get output, but lose visibility, control, and capability. A GCC is your own entity: your people, your culture, your intellectual property, your data. The talent works for you, builds deep institutional knowledge of your business, and becomes a genuine strategic asset over time. Outsourcing is faster to start and lower in fixed cost, but a GCC compounds in value in ways outsourcing cannot. Many multinationals that outsourced aggressively in the 2000s and 2010s are now setting up GCCs to recapture the capability they gave away.

How much does it cost to set up a GCC in India?

Setup costs vary enormously based on city, scale, function mix, and entity structure. A lean GCC setup in a Tier 2 city can begin with an initial investment of USD 500K to USD 1M covering legal, leadership, real estate, and first-year operations. A large-scale GCC in Bangalore with 500+ headcount and advanced infrastructure can require USD 10M or more in initial capital. The more important number is the business case — a well-designed GCC typically delivers 40 to 60% cost savings versus equivalent onshore operations within 3 years, alongside significant quality and capability gains. An experienced GCC advisory consultant can help you model this accurately before you commit.

Do I need a GCC consultant, or can we set this up ourselves?

Technically, you can set up a GCC without external advisory support. Many companies try. The ones that succeed typically have a senior leader who has done it before, a dedicated internal team, and a well-connected local network in India. Most multinational teams setting up their first GCC lack all three. A GCC consultant — particularly one who has actually built and run a GCC — dramatically compresses the learning curve, prevents expensive structural mistakes, and provides access to a network that takes years to build. The cost of good advisory is a fraction of the cost of a poorly-structured GCC.
